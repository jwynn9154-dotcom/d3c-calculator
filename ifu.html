The D3C Calculator estimates renal function in the early post-kidney-transplant period, when serum creatinine is still falling and standard CKD-EPI underestimates true GFR. It is built for the first several days after transplant, before creatinine reaches a new steady state.

What it is for

Pharmacy uses the calculator to dose renally cleared drugs while creatinine is changing. The primary output, D3C* in mL/min, is an unindexed creatinine clearance that matches the units in package-insert dosing tables, so it slots into the same workflow as Cockcroft-Gault. Valganciclovir, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, vancomycin, and cefepime are the common targets in this window.

Nephrology can use the D3C eGFR (mL/min/1.73 m²) column to read the value on the same scale as the patient's eventual steady-state eGFR.

The method comes from Pieters et al., Clin Pharmacokinet 59:1303-1311, 2020, which found D3C more accurate than the Jelliffe and kinetic-eGFR formulas in transplant recipients.

Who and when

Use it for adult kidney transplant recipients with functioning grafts in roughly the first week post-op, while daily creatinine is dropping by more than about 5 to 10 percent. Once the daily change falls below that, the patient is effectively at steady state and standard CKD-EPI is appropriate again. The dynamic and static columns will converge at that point.

Do not use it for patients with delayed graft function who are dialysis-dependent. Creatinine is not a valid clearance marker while dialysis is removing it.

It is an adult tool. The production-rate term is not valid in children.

How to use it

  1. Enter age, sex, dry weight, and height.
  2. Enter the transplant date and time. Any creatinine drawn within 24 hours of that time is flagged low-confidence because of perioperative fluid shifts.
  3. If the patient is volume-overloaded, enter the current weight. Volume of distribution picks up the extra fluid; the production rate stays anchored to dry weight.
  4. Enter serial creatinines with their draw date and time. Each interval needs two values, so the first row has no result.

Read D3C* (mL/min) for drug dosing. Compare it against the static CKD-EPI column to see how much the standard equation is lagging.

Cautions

Each interval should span 4 to 48 hours. Shorter intervals have a poor signal-to-noise ratio; longer ones strain the assumption that GFR is constant across the interval. The calculator flags intervals outside that range.

If creatinine is rising rather than falling, the formula still computes, but the constant-GFR assumption is weaker. Tighten sampling to every 8 to 12 hours when working up possible allograft injury. Intervals with a rise over 10 percent are flagged.

The calculator estimates total renal clearance and does not separate graft function from any residual native function.

Local validation

Before clinical use, validate against your own data. Living-donor recipients with early graft function and a first-week timed urine collection are the cleanest comparison group. Pieters reported a mean prediction error near zero against 24-hour urinary creatinine clearance, so similar local agreement is the bar to clear before relying on the tool.
